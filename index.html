6th

6th Grade: The Beginning (And Almost The End)

First quarter of 6th grade. I picked up the flute and immediately regretted every decision that led me to that moment. The squeaks were constant. My embouchure — the way your lips shape on the mouthpiece — was completely wrong and I had no idea how to fix it. I'd watch other kids get their first clean notes and feel this pit in my stomach.

Here's the thing nobody tells you: flute is one of the hardest instruments to get a first clean tone on. It's not like a recorder where you just blow through a hole. The angle, the lip position, the air speed — everything has to sync up exactly. When it doesn't, you squeak. And when you squeak in front of your class every single day, it starts to feel personal.

I almost quit. I'm not embarrassed to say that. But I loved the flute too much to walk away — the way it looks, the way it sounds when someone actually knows how to play it. I made a deal with myself: one more month. And in that month, I found the fix for my embouchure. Everything changed from there.

7th

7th Grade: 10th Chair — My Best Motivation

Seventh grade chair auditions. I got 10th. Out of the flute section. I remember looking at that list and feeling the embarrassment wash over me. 10th chair. In the back.

But here's what happened next — and this is the part I want you to remember: I decided that 10th chair was information, not a verdict. It told me exactly where my weak spots were. My sight-reading was shaky. My tone in the upper register was inconsistent. My dynamics — the soft-to-loud control — needed serious work.

Instead of feeling sorry for myself, I made a list. Every single weakness, written down. I started working through them one by one. This is Logos — the logical approach — and it's what I'll teach you in our sessions. You don't try to fix everything at once. You identify your #1 problem and you destroy it. Then you move to the next one.

7th grade taught me how to learn. That's a skill nobody's born with — it's built. And I'm going to help you build it too.

☀️

The Summer That Changed Everything

Between 7th and 8th grade, I locked in. This is the summer plan that I'm going to give you in full detail in the Summer Lock-In section below — but here's the core of what I did:

  • 20 minutes every single day, no exceptions — not marathon 2-hour sessions that burn you out, but consistent, focused 20-minute blocks where every minute actually counted.
  • Recorded myself constantly — your ear lies to you. When you record and play it back, you hear what the audience actually hears. Game-changer.
  • Drilled my worst notes obsessively — not my best, my worst. The notes I hated playing. I played them slowly, perfectly, until they stopped feeling scary.
  • Listened to professional flutists every day — your ears need to know what "right" sounds like before your lips can get there.

The summer plan isn't complicated. But it requires doing it. That's the part I'll help you stay accountable on.

Summer Lock-In

The Plan That Changed Everything.

This is the summer plan that took me from 10th chair to First Chair, All-County, All-District, and Europe. Now it's yours.

Between 7th and 8th grade, I made a promise to myself: this summer is different. Not "I'll practice when I feel like it" different. Structured, intentional, locked in different. The result? I walked back into school in August and could feel that everything had shifted. Here is the exact plan — broken down so you can run it yourself starting right now.

01

The 20-Minute Daily Rule

This is non-negotiable and non-optional. Every single day — including weekends, including vacation days, including days where you really don't feel like it. Twenty minutes. Focused. No phone in reach.

The reason 20 minutes works better than "an hour on the weekends" is neurological: your brain builds musical muscle memory through consistent, repeated exposure — not marathon sessions. Daily 20-minute practice literally rewires your brain faster than sporadic long sessions. I'm not making that up — it's how skill development works.

Here's what those 20 minutes look like: 5 minutes of long tones (pure tone building), 5 minutes of scales (technique), 5 minutes on your current piece, and 5 minutes on your weakest area. That's the exact structure. Simple. Powerful. Repeatable.

02

Hunt Your Weak Notes

Most students practice their strongest stuff. They run through the parts they already know because it feels good. It sounds like progress. It isn't. Real progress lives in the uncomfortable places — the notes that make you cringe, the passages you always trip over, the high register that never quite speaks cleanly.

My system: at the start of every week, I identify my single biggest weakness. One thing. That thing gets attacked every single practice session until it's no longer a weakness. Then I find the next one. This method is why I improved so dramatically in one summer — instead of being "kinda okay at everything," I became genuinely strong in specific areas, stacking strength on top of strength.

The high D that always cracked? Gone. The G-flat that never wanted to speak? Handled. The ritardando section that fell apart at tempo? Conquered. One at a time. Every week.

03

Record Yourself. Always.

This is the hack that surprised me the most. Your ears, while you're playing, lie to you. Your brain fills in what you expect to hear, not what's actually coming out of the instrument. You think you sound great. You hit record, play it back, and realize you've been slightly out of tune for months.

Recording yourself fixes this instantly. Use your phone. Record every practice session, or at least one run-through per session. Play it back with headphones. Listen as if you're a judge hearing a stranger. You will immediately hear things you cannot hear while playing.

I started doing this halfway through my summer plan and it was like turning on a light in a dark room. Every small mistake that I'd been missing — intonation drift on long notes, rushed sixteenth notes, inconsistent tone in the upper register — became visible (or rather, audible) immediately. Fix what you hear. Record again. Repeat.

04

Listen Like a Musician

Every single day of that summer, I listened to professional flutists. James Galway. Emmanuel Pahud. Jasmine Choi. Not background music — active, focused listening where I paid attention to how they shaped phrases, where they breathed, how their tone changed from soft to loud.

Here's why this matters: your brain needs to hear "right" before your body can produce "right." The reason most beginners struggle with tone is that they've never actually listened closely enough to an expert to know what they're aiming for. Once you have that sonic target locked in your head, your body will start moving toward it instinctively.

Even 10 minutes a day of intentional listening to pro flutists will rewire the way you hear music — and the way you approach your instrument. This is the quietest item on this list and possibly the most powerful.
