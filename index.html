6th

6th Grade: The Worst Squeaks You've Ever Heard

I picked up the flute in 6th grade and immediately regretted it. The squeaks were nonstop. My embouchure — the shape your lips make on the mouthpiece — was completely wrong, and I had zero idea how to fix it. I'd watch everyone else get clean notes while I sat there making sounds that made people flinch.

Here's the thing nobody tells you: flute is one of the hardest instruments to get your first real sound on. Your lip angle, position, and airspeed all have to sync up perfectly at the same moment. Until they do, you squeak — in front of everyone, every single day.

I almost quit. I'm being completely real. But I gave myself one more month — and in that month, I cracked it. That was the moment everything changed.

7th

7th Grade: 10th Chair. My Real Wake-Up Call.

Chair auditions, 7th grade. I checked the list. 10th chair — dead last in the flute section. Ngl, that one stung.

But here's what I did instead of feeling sorry for myself: I turned that placement into a to-do list. 10th chair showed me my exact weak spots — shaky sight-reading, weak high notes, zero control over dynamics. I wrote every weakness down, then started destroying them one by one.

That's the move. Your chair placement isn't a judgment on you — it's a map of exactly what to fix. I'll teach you how to use it the same way I did.

Summer Lock-In

The Plan That Changed Everything.

10th chair to First Chair, All-County, All-District, and Europe — in one summer. Here's exactly what I did, step by step.

The summer between 7th and 8th grade, I made myself one real promise: this summer is actually different. Not "I'll practice when I feel like it." Truly locked in, every single day, on purpose. I came back to school in August and could feel that something had genuinely shifted — in my sound, my confidence, and the way the flute felt in my hands. Here's the four things that made it happen.

01

20 Minutes. Every Single Day.

Not optional. Weekends. Vacation days. Days when you really, really don't want to. Twenty minutes. Phone in another room. Full focus.

Twenty focused minutes every day crushes two hours once a weekend, every single time. Your brain builds muscle memory through consistent daily repetition — not big, occasional bursts. Skipping days doesn't just pause progress — it starts undoing it.

My split: 5 min long tones · 5 min scales · 5 min on your piece · 5 min attacking your worst weakness.

02

Attack Your Weakest Spot First.

Most people practice the stuff they're already good at because it feels nice. That's not growth. That's just staying comfortable. Real improvement lives in the uncomfortable spots.

Every week I picked my single worst weakness and attacked it every session until it wasn't a weakness anymore. High D cracking? Attacked it. G-flat refusing to speak? Attacked it. Slow passage falling apart at tempo? Attacked it. One problem per week. Stack the wins. That's the actual formula.

03

Record Yourself. Every Session.

This was the biggest game-changer and the most surprising one. Here's the thing: your brain tricks you while you're playing. It fills in what you expect to hear, not what's actually coming out. You think you sound solid. You hit record, play it back, and suddenly hear a completely different story.

Use your phone. Record a full run-through every session. Listen back like you're a judge hearing a stranger play. Fix what you hear. Record again. This one habit alone will make you improve faster than almost anything else on this list.

04

Listen to the Best Players Every Day.

Every day that summer, I listened to world-class flutists — James Galway, Emmanuel Pahud, Jasmine Choi. Not as background noise. Active listening — really paying attention to their tone, their phrasing, where they breathe, how they shape every note.

Your ears need a target before your hands can aim for it. You literally cannot produce a sound you've never truly heard at a high level. Ten minutes of focused listening daily is one of the most powerful things on this entire list — and the easiest to start right now.
