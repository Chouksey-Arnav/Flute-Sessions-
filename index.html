6th

6th Grade: The Squeaks Were Real

I picked up the flute in 6th grade and immediately questioned everything. The squeaks were relentless. My embouchure — the precise lip position that produces sound — was completely wrong and I had no idea how to correct it. I watched everyone around me get clean tones while I sat there producing sounds that made people wince.

Here's what nobody tells beginners: the flute is one of the hardest instruments to get your first real sound on. Your lip angle, position, and airspeed all have to align perfectly at the same moment. Until they do, you squeak — in front of everyone, every day.

I gave myself one more month before walking away. In that month, I cracked it. That was the turning point that changed everything.

7th

7th Grade: 10th Chair. My Real Wake-Up Call.

Chair auditions, 7th grade. I checked the list. 10th chair — last in the entire flute section. That stung. A lot.

But instead of giving up, I turned that placement into a roadmap. 10th chair showed me my exact weaknesses: shaky sight-reading, thin high notes, zero command over dynamics. I wrote every one down, then started systematically eliminating them, one by one.

That's the move. Your chair placement isn't a judgment on your potential — it's a precise list of what to fix next. I'll show you how to use it exactly the way I did.

☀️

The Summer I Decided to Be Great

Between 7th and 8th grade, I made one real decision: no more unfocused practice. Every single day, I ran the same four-step system:

  • 20 focused minutes every day — no skipping, no excuses, phone in another room.
  • Recorded every single session — your brain lies about how good you sound. The recording always tells the truth.
  • Drilled my worst notes first — slowly and perfectly, until they stopped being scary.
  • Listened to pro flutists every day — you can't play something you've never truly heard done right.

That complete system is free. I break it down step by step in the Summer Lock-In section below.

Summer Lock-In

The Plan That Changed Everything.

10th chair to First Chair, All-County, All-District, and Europe — all in one summer. Here's exactly what I did, every day.

The summer between 7th and 8th grade, I made myself one real promise: this summer is actually different. Not "I'll practice when I feel like it." Truly locked in, daily, with intention. I came back in August and could feel that something had genuinely changed — in my sound, my confidence, and the way the flute felt in my hands. These are the four things that made it happen.

01

20 Minutes. Every Single Day.

Not optional. Weekends, vacation days, days where you really don't feel like it — none of it matters. Twenty minutes. Phone in another room. Full focus.

Twenty focused daily minutes demolishes two hours on the weekend, every time. Your brain builds muscle memory through consistent repetition, not big occasional bursts. Daily practice literally rewires how you play. Skip days and you start undoing the gains.

My split: 5 min long tones · 5 min scales · 5 min on your piece · 5 min attacking your worst weakness.

02

Attack Your Weakest Spot First.

Most players practice what they're already decent at because it feels good. That's not progress — that's just staying comfortable. Real growth lives in the hard, uncomfortable places.

Every week I found my single worst weakness and attacked it every session until it stopped being a weakness. High D cracking? Attacked it. G-flat not speaking? Attacked it. Passage falling apart at tempo? Attacked it. One thing per week. Stack the wins. That's the real formula.

03

Record Yourself. Every Session.

This was the biggest game-changer and the most surprising one. Here's the truth: your brain lies to you while you play. It fills in what you expect to hear instead of what's actually coming out. You think you sound solid. You hit record, play it back, and hear a completely different story.

Use your phone. Record one full run-through per session. Listen back like you're a judge hearing a stranger. Fix what you hear. Record again. This loop alone will make you improve faster than almost anything else.

04

Listen to the Best Players Every Day.

Every day that summer, I listened to world-class flutists — James Galway, Emmanuel Pahud, Jasmine Choi. Not as background noise. Intentional, focused listening — paying real attention to their tone, breath, and how they shape each phrase.

Your ears need a target before your hands can aim at it. You cannot produce a sound you've never truly heard at a high level. Ten minutes of focused daily listening is quietly one of the most powerful things on this entire list.
