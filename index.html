6th

6th Grade: The Squeaks Were Unreal 😬

I picked up the flute in 6th grade and immediately questioned everything. The squeaks were relentless. My embouchure — the precise lip shape that produces sound — was completely wrong, and I had zero idea how to correct it. Everyone around me played actual notes while I made sounds that made people wince.

Here's what nobody tells 6th graders: the flute is one of the hardest instruments to get your first real tone on. Lip angle, position, and airspeed all sync up at once or nothing works. Until they do, you squeak in front of everyone, every day.

I almost quit. But I gave myself one more month. In that month, I cracked it. That crack was the turning point that led to everything else.

🔓 6th Grade Unlocks Consistent tone → Chair audition confidence → First competition eligibility
7th

7th Grade: 10th Out of 16 — My Wake-Up Call 😤

Chair auditions, 7th grade. I checked the list: 10th chair out of 16 flutists — a motivating challenge that showed me my exact weak spots with brutal precision.

Instead of feeling defeated, I turned that placement into a precise improvement plan. 10th out of 16 told me everything: inconsistent sight-reading, thin high notes, zero dynamic range. Every weakness became a target. Every challenge was a step toward mastery.

Your chair placement is not a verdict on your potential. It's a map of exactly what to fix. I'll show you how to read that map and use it exactly the way I did.

🔓 7th Grade Unlocks Chair improvements → All-County → All-District → All-State pathway
☀️

The Summer I Decided to Be Great 🔒

Between 7th and 8th grade, I made one real decision: no more unfocused practice. Every day, I ran the same four-step system:

  • 20 focused minutes every day — phone in another room, no exceptions.
  • Recorded every session — your brain lies about how you sound. A recording never does.
  • Drilled my worst notes first — slowly, perfectly, until they stopped being scary.
  • Listened to pro flutists daily — you can't produce a sound you've never truly heard.

That complete system is in the Summer Lock-In section below. Don't skip it.

Summer Lock-In

The Plan That Changed Everything.

10th of 16 → First Chair → All-County → All-District → Europe. One summer. Here is every single thing I did — adapted for 6th, 7th, and 8th graders.

The summer between 7th and 8th grade I made myself one real promise: this summer is actually different. No more "I'll practice when motivated." Truly locked in, every single day, with a plan. I came back in August and could feel something had genuinely shifted — in my sound, my confidence, and the way the flute felt like an extension of myself. These four pillars made it happen. And they work whether you're going into 7th grade, 8th grade, or entering 6th for the first time.

01

Daily Practice — Every. Single. Day. ⏰

Not optional. Weekends. Vacation days. Days you genuinely don't feel like it. Phone in another room. Full focus. No exceptions.

Daily repetition destroys occasional marathon sessions every time. Your brain builds musical muscle memory through consistency — not bursts. Skip days and you undo your own gains.

💚 6th Grade: 15 min — 5 tone · 5 notes · 5 worst spot
💙 7th Grade: 20 min — 5 scales · 5 sight-read · 5 piece · 5 weakness
⭐ 8th Grade: 20 min — 5 long tones · 5 scales · 5 piece · 5 solo artistry
02

Attack Your Worst Spot First. 🎯

Most players practice what they're already decent at because it feels good. That's not growth — that's comfort. Real mastery lives in the uncomfortable places.

Every week I identified my single worst weakness and attacked it every session until it stopped being a weakness. High D cracking? Attacked it. G-flat refusing to speak? Attacked it. One problem per week. Stack the wins. That's the formula.

03

Record Yourself Every Session. 📱

This was the single biggest game-changer. Here's the truth: your brain lies to you while you play. It fills in what you expect to hear rather than what's actually coming out.

Use your phone. Record one full run-through per session. Listen back like you're a judge hearing a stranger. Fix what you hear. Record again. This loop alone accelerates improvement faster than almost anything else on this list.

04

Listen to the Best Players Every Day. 🎧

Every day that summer, I listened to world-class flutists — James Galway, Emmanuel Pahud, Jasmine Choi. Not as background noise. Active, intentional listening — paying close attention to tone, breath, and how they shape every phrase.

Your ears need a target before your hands can aim at one. You cannot produce a sound you have never truly heard at a high level. Ten focused minutes of daily listening is one of the most underrated habits on this list.
