My Journey

Every struggle. Every turning point. Every lesson I had to learn the hard way — documented so you don't have to repeat my mistakes.

6th

6th Grade: The Squeaks Were Real

First semester of 6th grade. I picked up the flute and immediately questioned every choice that led me there. The squeaks were constant. My embouchure — the precise lip position required to produce sound — was completely wrong, and I didn't know how to fix it.

Here's what no one tells beginners: the flute is one of the most technically demanding instruments to get an initial sound on. Lip angle, airspeed, and position must all align perfectly at once. Until they do, you squeak — in front of everyone, every day.

I gave myself one more month before walking away. In that month, I cracked the code. That was the turning point.

7th

7th Grade: 10th Chair Was My Wake-Up Call

Chair auditions, 7th grade. I checked the results. 10th chair — last in the flute section. I'm not going to pretend that didn't sting.

But instead of feeling sorry for myself, I turned that list into a roadmap. 10th chair told me exactly what I needed to work on: inconsistent sight-reading, weak upper register, and no control over dynamics. I wrote every weakness down, then started eliminating them one by one, in order.

Your chair placement isn't a judgment. It's a starting point. I'll show you how to use it exactly the way I did.

☀️

The Summer I Decided to Get Serious

Between 7th and 8th grade, I committed to a daily plan — no exceptions. Four disciplines, every single day:

  • 20 focused minutes daily — no phone, no distractions, no excuses.
  • Recorded every session — your brain fills in what it expects. A recording reveals what's actually there.
  • Drilled my weakest notes first — slowly, precisely, until they no longer intimidated me.
  • Listened to professional flutists daily — you cannot produce a sound you've never truly heard.

The complete version of that plan is free in the Summer Lock-In section below.

Summer Lock-In

The Plan That Changed Everything.

From 10th chair to First Chair, All-County, All-District, and Europe — in a single summer. Here is exactly what I did.

The summer between 7th and 8th grade, I made a real commitment: this summer is different. Not "I'll practice when I feel motivated." Locked in. Consistent. Deliberate. I returned to school in August and could immediately feel the difference — in my tone, my confidence, and my command of the instrument. Here are the four things that made that happen.

01

20 Minutes. Every Single Day.

Non-negotiable. Weekends, vacations, days when motivation is zero. Twenty minutes. Phone out of reach. Full attention.

Consistent daily practice builds musical muscle memory far more effectively than long, infrequent sessions. Your brain retains skills through repetition, not volume. Daily practice literally changes how your brain processes music. Miss days, and you undo the gains.

My daily structure: 5 min long tones · 5 min scales · 5 min repertoire · 5 min attacking your weakest area.

02

Attack Your Weakest Area First.

Most students gravitate toward what they already play well. It feels productive. It isn't. Real progress happens in the places that are uncomfortable.

Every week, I identified my single worst weakness and targeted it every session until it was no longer a weakness. High D cracking? Targeted. G-flat not speaking? Targeted. Slow passage falling apart at tempo? Targeted. One thing per week. Stack improvements. That is the actual formula.

03

Record Yourself. Every Session.

This was the single biggest revelation of my summer. Here's the truth: your brain deceives you while you play. It replaces what you hear with what it expects to hear. You assume you sound solid. Then you play the recording back and discover a completely different reality.

Use your phone. Record one clean run-through per session. Listen back critically — as if you were a judge hearing a stranger. Fix every flaw you identify. Record again. This single habit will accelerate your progress more than almost anything else.

04

Listen to the Best Players Every Day.

Every day that summer, I listened to professional flutists — James Galway, Emmanuel Pahud, Jasmine Choi. Not as background music. Active, deliberate listening — studying their tone quality, phrasing, and breath control.

Your ears need a clear target before your hands can aim for it. You cannot consistently produce a sound you have never truly heard at a high level. Ten minutes of intentional daily listening is one of the most underestimated tools available to any student musician.
